1. 緒論1. Introduction

1.1 研究背景1.1 Background

「美股長期年化報酬 10%」是投資界最廣為流傳的數字之一。在 YouTube、Podcast 和各類投資課程中， 這個數字被反覆引用，經常伴隨著「只要長期持有美股，就能穩定獲得 10% 的年化報酬」的暗示。 這個說法的根源是 S&P 500 指數自 1926 年以來的歷史表現。

然而，這個看似簡單的「10%」背後隱藏著多個重要的細節：它是名目報酬還是實質報酬？ 是否包含股息再投入？起始年份的選擇如何影響結果？對於非美國投資人，匯率風險又如何？

更根本的問題是：以美國這個 20 世紀最成功的經濟體為樣本，推論「股市長期報酬 10%」， 是否存在嚴重的存活偏差（Survivorship Bias）？

"US stocks return 10% annually over the long term" is one of the most widely cited figures in investing. Across YouTube, podcasts, and investment courses, this number is repeatedly referenced, often implying that simply holding US stocks long-term guarantees ~10% annualized returns. The origin traces to the S&P 500's historical performance since 1926.

However, several critical details hide behind this simple "10%": Is it nominal or real? Does it include dividend reinvestment? How does the starting year affect results? For non-US investors, what about currency risk?

More fundamentally: Using the most successful 20th-century economy as a sample to claim "stocks return 10% long-term" — does this suffer from serious survivorship bias?

1.2 文獻回顧1.2 Literature Review

Siegel (2014)《Stocks for the Long Run》研究美股 1802-2012 的數據， 發現實質年化報酬約 6.6%，而非名目的 10%。通膨的長期侵蝕使得「10%」的購買力遠不如表面數字。

Dimson, Marsh & Staunton (2002)《Triumph of the Optimists》分析全球 16 國 1900-2000 年 的股市表現，發現實質報酬中位數約 5%，美國位居前列。這意味著以美國為代表來推論「全球股市」有嚴重偏差。

Robert Shiller 的 CAPE 數據集提供了 1871 年至今的月頻數據， 包含價格、股息、盈餘與 CPI，是驗證美股長期報酬最完整的公開數據源。

Siegel (2014) in "Stocks for the Long Run" studied US stock data from 1802-2012, finding real annualized returns of approximately 6.6%, not the nominal 10%. Long-term inflation erosion means the purchasing power of "10%" is far less than the headline number suggests.

Dimson, Marsh & Staunton (2002) in "Triumph of the Optimists" analyzed stock markets in 16 countries from 1900-2000, finding median real returns of approximately 5%, with the US near the top. This implies significant bias in using the US as representative of "global stock markets."

Robert Shiller's CAPE dataset provides monthly data from 1871 to present, including price, dividends, earnings, and CPI — the most comprehensive public data source for verifying US long-term stock returns.
